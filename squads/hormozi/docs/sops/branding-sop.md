## Part 1: Purpose

Build a brand that commands premium prices, attracts customers at lower costs, and creates lifelong loyalty. Branding is the deliberate pairing of your business with things your ideal customer likes.

**Core Definition:**
```
Branding = Deliberate pairing of things
Good Branding = Pairing with things your ideal audience LIKES
Bad Branding = Pairing with things your audience DISLIKES
No Branding = Exposure results in normal behavior (invisible)
```

**Business Impact:**
```
Brand enables:
1) Lower CAC - Convert higher % from advertising at lower cost
2) Higher LTV - Charge 2x, 5x, or 10x more for the same thing
3) Lower Risk - Customers buy repeatedly without considering competition

"The only way to make all three happen, for the lowest cost,
over the longest time horizon is one word: branding." (p.3)
```

**Brand Measurement Variables:**
```
Advertising = REACH (how many people know about you)
Branding = INFLUENCE (chance it changes behavior) + DIRECTION (towards or away)

Good advertising = Everyone knows about your stuff
Good branding = Audience likes and BUYS
```

## Part 2: Scope

### When This SOP Applies
- Building a brand from scratch (new business, new product)
- Growing an existing brand (expanding reach and influence)
- Pivoting a damaged or misaligned brand
- Repositioning for a new market segment (up/down/adjacent/broader/narrower)

### When This SOP Does NOT Apply
- Direct response advertising tactics (covered in separate playbook)
- Product development (separate from brand positioning)
- Pricing strategy (brand enables pricing, doesn't set it)

### Business Types
- Personal brands (entrepreneurs, thought leaders)
- Company brands (products, services)
- Any business wanting premium pricing power
- B2B and B2C

## Part 4: The Four-Step Brand Framework

### Framework Overview

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              FOUR STEPS TO BUILD, GROW, OR PIVOT A BRAND                │
├──────────────┬──────────────┬──────────────────┬───────────────────────┤
│   STEP 1     │    STEP 2    │     STEP 3       │        STEP 4         │
│   WHO        │    WHAT      │     ASSOCIATE    │       OPTIMIZE        │
├──────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────────────────┤
│ Define ideal │ Figure out   │ Associate with   │ Optimize for          │
│ customer     │ what they    │ those things     │ max profit            │
│              │ like         │                  │                       │
├──────────────┼──────────────┼──────────────────┼───────────────────────┤
│ Growing      │ Values       │ Content          │ Up market             │
│ Money        │ People       │ Products         │ Down market           │
│ Easy to find │ Outcomes     │ Appearances      │ Adjacent              │
│ In pain      │ Experiences  │                  │ Broader/Narrower      │
└──────────────┴──────────────┴──────────────────┴───────────────────────┘
```

### The Branding Cycle (Levels of Authority)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    LEVELS OF AUTHORITY (BRANDING CYCLE)                  │
├────────────────────┬────────────────────┬───────────────────────────────┤
│  1. WHAT YOU SAY   │ 2. WHAT OTHERS SAY │ 3. WHAT THEY EXPERIENCE      │
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Least influential  │ More influential   │ MOST influential              │
│ Content, Ads,      │ Affiliates,        │ Product experience,           │
│ Outreach           │ Customers          │ Personal opinion              │
├────────────────────┼────────────────────┼───────────────────────────────┤
│ Short-term impact  │ Medium-term impact │ Long-term impact              │
│ on brand           │ on brand           │ on brand                      │
└────────────────────┴────────────────────┴───────────────────────────────┘

"Your advertising has stronger influence on your brand in the short term.
Your product has stronger influence on your brand in the long term." (p.24)
```

### The Bouquet Metaphor

```
Brand = Bouquet of Flowers

Individual elements (products, values, experiences, people) = individual flowers
Assembled together = brand bouquet

Narrow brand = Single type of flowers (e.g., only red roses)
Broad brand = Many types of flowers
Adjacent move = Shift from red roses to yellow roses
Bad pairing = Sticks and weeds in bouquet (distorts clarity)
```

## Part 5: Process Steps

### STEP 1: FIGURE OUT WHO YOUR IDEAL CUSTOMER IS

#### Step 1.1: Identify Growing Market
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Select an audience that is growing, not shrinking or maintaining |
| **Cognitive Type** | Analysis (market research) |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM - Data gathering automatable, selection requires judgment |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Access to market data |
| **Output** | Market growth assessment |
| **Guardrails** | "If I have to pick between shrinking, maintaining, or growing - I'd rather go after one that's bigger" (p.20) |
| **Decision Rule** | Growing market > Stable market > Shrinking market |

**Rationale:** "Even if I stay the same size, I'm still going to grow because the market's growing." (p.20)

#### Step 1.2: Verify Financial Capacity
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Ensure target audience can afford what you sell |
| **Cognitive Type** | Analysis |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM - Income data accessible |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Pricing range defined |
| **Output** | Audience affordability assessment |
| **Guardrails** | "If I want to charge a lot of money, they've got to have the money so I can charge it" (p.20) |
| **Decision Rule** | Rich people > Medium people > Poor people (for premium offerings) |

#### Step 1.3: Assess Targeting Ease
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Determine how easily you can reach this audience with advertising |
| **Cognitive Type** | Analysis |
| **Automation Potential** | HIGH - Platform targeting data accessible |
| **Executor** | Worker |
| **Precondition** | Advertising platforms identified |
| **Output** | Targeting feasibility score |
| **Guardrails** | "If my customers exist, but I can't find them... does it matter?" (p.20) |
| **Decision Rule** | Easy to target > Hard to target |

#### Step 1.4: Evaluate Pain Level
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Confirm audience desperately wants what you sell |
| **Cognitive Type** | Judgment + Perception |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW - Requires qualitative research |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Offer defined |
| **Output** | Pain intensity assessment |
| **Guardrails** | "I'd rather have competition because at least I know people are buying that thing" (p.20) |
| **Decision Rule** | Desperate pain > Moderate discomfort > Mild inconvenience |

**Four Criteria Summary:**
```
Ideal Avatar Checklist:
[ ] Growing market
[ ] Can afford your stuff
[ ] Easy to find/target
[ ] In significant pain
```

### STEP 2: FIGURE OUT WHAT THEY LIKE

#### Step 2.1: Identify Values They Hold
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Research and document core values your audience prizes |
| **Cognitive Type** | Synthesis + Perception |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM - Survey data automatable, interpretation human |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Avatar defined (Step 1) |
| **Output** | Values inventory |
| **Guardrails** | Focus on values that differentiate, not universal ones |
| **Decision Rule** | Prioritize values that connect to your offering |

**Example (Assisted Living Owners):**
- Making more money
- Getting more customers
- Making more profits
- Building a sellable business
- Fewer lawsuits
- Paying fewer taxes (p.21)

#### Step 2.2: Identify People They Admire
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Document people, figures, and authorities your audience respects |
| **Cognitive Type** | Research + Synthesis |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Avatar defined |
| **Output** | Admired figures inventory |
| **Guardrails** | Verify admiration, not just awareness |
| **Decision Rule** | Strong positive association > Mild positive > Neutral |

**Example:**
- "We'll pair Elon and Warren Buffet, a couple of the richest people in the world. People who like investing, entrepreneurship, and money would probably respond favorably." (p.14)

#### Step 2.3: Identify Outcomes They Desire
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Document the specific results and transformations they want |
| **Cognitive Type** | Synthesis |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW - Requires deep customer understanding |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Avatar in pain (Step 1.4) |
| **Output** | Desired outcomes inventory |
| **Guardrails** | Outcomes must be concrete and measurable |
| **Decision Rule** | Specific outcomes > Vague aspirations |

#### Step 2.4: Identify Experiences They Value
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Document experiences, activities, and consumption preferences |
| **Cognitive Type** | Research |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Avatar defined |
| **Output** | Experience preferences inventory |
| **Guardrails** | Include both aspirational and current experiences |
| **Decision Rule** | Experiences that connect to brand message |

### STEP 3: ASSOCIATE YOUR BRAND WITH THINGS THEY LIKE

#### Step 3.1: Create Content They Value
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Make videos, blogs, articles, booklets, PDFs geared toward audience |
| **Cognitive Type** | Creativity + Execution |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW - Creative work requires human |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Values and interests documented (Step 2) |
| **Output** | Content library |
| **Guardrails** | Content must genuinely help audience; no filler |
| **Decision Rule** | "When I start doing this, people are like, 'Wow, this stuff really makes my life better'" (p.22) |

**Content Examples (Assisted Living Law Firm):**
- "Here are some things that we see across our firm for Assisted Living facilities that have helped lower costs and improve quality of life for residents." (p.22)
- "They can structure their accounting to depreciate their investments this way so they can save more money." (p.22)
- "Here's how people set up their floor plans to make it more efficient." (p.22)

#### Step 3.2: Distribute Content Widely
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Get content in front of target audience through multiple channels |
| **Cognitive Type** | Execution + Analysis |
| **Automation Potential** | HIGH - Distribution automatable |
| **Executor** | Worker |
| **Precondition** | Content created (Step 3.1) |
| **Output** | Reach metrics |
| **Guardrails** | Track which channels resonate with avatar |
| **Decision Rule** | More reach = more brand building (reach is primary brand element) |

**Key Insight:** "A brand can't exist without [reach], so reach is the primary element of a brand." (p.8)

#### Step 3.3: Create Products They Like
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Develop both free and paid offerings that serve the audience |
| **Cognitive Type** | Synthesis + Creativity |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Values documented |
| **Output** | Product/offering suite |
| **Guardrails** | Products must match brand promises |
| **Decision Rule** | "Your premium brand had better be delivering something special" - Warren Buffett (p.24) |

#### Step 3.4: Appear Next to People They Like
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Associate with admired figures, participate in valued communities |
| **Cognitive Type** | Judgment + Negotiation |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Admired figures identified (Step 2.2) |
| **Output** | Association portfolio |
| **Guardrails** | Associations must be authentic; forced pairings backfire |
| **Decision Rule** | Strong positive associations > Weak positive > None |

**Association Transfer Mechanism:**
```
1) Start with brand that means nothing (just a logo)
2) Pair it with stuff your customers like
3) Brand starts to mean "thing customer likes" to them
4) Add brand to generic product → transforms to premium branded product
5) Customers want to associate with that positive branded product
6) They exchange money for premium product
7) They get product AND association
8) You get higher profits from those associations (p.16-17)
```

#### Step 3.5: Iterate Based on Response
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Monitor audience response and adjust associations accordingly |
| **Cognitive Type** | Analysis + Judgment |
| **Automation Potential** | MEDIUM - Data automatable, decisions human |
| **Executor** | Hybrid |
| **Precondition** | Content distributed, feedback loop exists |
| **Output** | Optimized association strategy |
| **Guardrails** | Measure behavior change, not just sentiment |
| **Decision Rule** | What changes buying behavior = good; what doesn't = adjust |

### STEP 4: OPTIMIZE ASSOCIATIONS FOR MAXIMUM PROFIT

#### Step 4.1: Assess Market Position Options
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Evaluate five directions for expanding addressable market |
| **Cognitive Type** | Strategic Analysis |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Current brand established |
| **Output** | Market positioning recommendation |
| **Guardrails** | Each direction requires different associations |
| **Decision Rule** | Choose direction that maximizes profit potential |

**Five Positioning Options:**
```
1) UP MARKET - Larger customers
   Example: Multi-location assisted living owners

2) DOWN MARKET - Smaller customers
   Example: At-home care and mobile assisted living owners

3) ADJACENT - Similar but slightly different market
   Example: Rehab facilities

4) BROADER - Entire category
   Example: All senior living centers (hospice, end of life, retirement)

5) NARROWER - Slice of existing market
   Example: Assisted living with 100-500 residents (p.25)
```

#### Step 4.2: Execute Brand Pivot (If Needed)
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Shift associations to match new target market |
| **Cognitive Type** | Strategic Execution |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | New direction chosen (Step 4.1) |
| **Output** | Pivoted brand positioning |
| **Guardrails** | Must gain more than you lose |
| **Decision Rule** | "Net positive on all moves we make" (p.27) |

**Bouquet Adjustment Strategy:**
- Narrow: "Only bouquet of red roses" - talk only about specific things
- Broader: "All sorts of flowers" - expand topic range
- Adjacent: "Red roses to yellow roses" - still roses, different type
- Up market: Focus on problems larger players have
- Down market: Focus on problems smaller players have (p.25-26)

#### Step 4.3: Monitor Net Audience Changes
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Track whether moves gain more audience than they lose |
| **Cognitive Type** | Analysis |
| **Automation Potential** | HIGH - Metrics automatable |
| **Executor** | Worker |
| **Precondition** | Pivot executed |
| **Output** | Net change assessment |
| **Guardrails** | "Losing audience is a part of life. You just wanna make sure you gain more than you lose" (p.27) |
| **Decision Rule** | Positive net change = continue; Negative = revert |

**Case Study (Hormozi Personal Brand):**
"When I started branding, I only made business content. As I got bigger I started touching on other topics. Views followed. But, I realized it was the wrong views. So, I recentered my content around only business. And unsurprisingly, more business owners paid attention." (p.26)

#### Step 4.4: Recover from Branding Mistakes
| Field | Value |
|-------|-------|
| **Description** | Overwhelm bad pairings with good ones rather than trying to eliminate |
| **Cognitive Type** | Execution |
| **Automation Potential** | LOW |
| **Executor** | Human |
| **Precondition** | Bad pairing identified |
| **Output** | Recovered brand perception |
| **Guardrails** | "We don't try and eliminate the DUI... But what we try to do is overwhelm it with way more of the stuff that the majority of our people actually like" (p.29) |
| **Decision Rule** | More good associations > Hiding/eliminating bad |

**Recovery Mechanism:**
"Just like one ugly flower messes up the whole bouquet... To recover from something like that, you just have to overwhelm customers with the stuff they like until eventually the bad pairing shrinks into irrelevance." (p.29)

## Part 6: Decision Rules

### DR-001: Avatar Selection
```yaml
rule: AVATAR_QUALIFICATION
trigger: Selecting target audience
condition:
  - IF market_growing AND has_money AND easy_to_target AND in_pain THEN "IDEAL"
  - IF 3_of_4_criteria THEN "ACCEPTABLE"
  - IF <3_of_4_criteria THEN "RECONSIDER"
action: Prioritize ideal avatars
confidence: HIGH (p.19-21)
```

### DR-002: Association Quality
```yaml
rule: PAIRING_EVALUATION
trigger: Considering new brand association
condition:
  - IF majority_of_audience_likes THEN "GOOD_PAIRING"
  - IF majority_of_audience_dislikes THEN "BAD_PAIRING"
  - IF neutral_response THEN "NO_BRANDING" (invisible)
action: Only execute good pairings
confidence: HIGH (p.6)
```

### DR-003: Pivot Execution
```yaml
rule: BRAND_PIVOT_DECISION
trigger: Considering market repositioning
condition:
  - IF expected_gains > expected_losses THEN "EXECUTE_PIVOT"
  - IF expected_losses > expected_gains THEN "ABORT_PIVOT"
  - IF uncertain THEN "TEST_SMALL_FIRST"
action: Never make negative net changes
confidence: HIGH (p.27)
```

### DR-004: Mistake Recovery
```yaml
rule: BAD_PAIRING_RECOVERY
trigger: Brand damaged by bad association
condition:
  - IF bad_pairing_identified THEN overwhelm_with_good()
  - NEVER try_to_eliminate() or hide()
action: Double down on positive associations
confidence: HIGH (p.29)
```

### DR-005: Pricing Power Check
```yaml
rule: BRAND_STRENGTH_INDICATOR
trigger: Evaluating brand effectiveness
condition:
  - IF can_raise_prices_without_losing_business THEN "STRONG_BRAND"
  - IF need_prayer_session_before_10%_increase THEN "WEAK_BRAND"
action: Use as diagnostic for brand health
confidence: HIGH (Warren Buffett quote, p.17)
```

### DR-006: Channel Authority Weighting
```yaml
rule: INFLUENCE_SOURCE_PRIORITY
trigger: Allocating brand-building resources
condition:
  - experience_weight: HIGHEST (long-term impact)
  - others_say_weight: MEDIUM (referrals, affiliates)
  - you_say_weight: LOWEST (your advertising)
action: Invest most in product experience
confidence: HIGH (p.23-24)
```

## Part 7: Exceptions & Edge Cases

### Exception 1: High Reach, Low Influence
**Trigger:** Many people know about you but don't change behavior
**Example:** Taylor Swift brand for Hormozi - "I think she's good but probably won't buy much of her stuff" (p.9)
**Response:** Focus on deepening engagement with target niche rather than broader reach

### Exception 2: Polar vs Non-Polar Brand Decision
**Trigger:** Questioning whether to deliberately polarize
**Analysis:** "You don't have to be polar to build a strong brand... Just look at brands like Taylor Swift, Mother Teresa, Apple" (p.13)
**Response:** "Selling polarizes people. Deliberately trying to 'polarize' interferes with doing business - Just focus on getting people to buy your stuff" (p.13)

### Exception 3: Bad Publicity Management
**Trigger:** Negative publicity event
**Analysis:** "Good beats bad. Bad beats none. Lots of good beats everything." (p.12)
**Response:** Don't shrink away; double down on positive associations

### Exception 4: Misaligned Audience Growth
**Trigger:** Growing audience but wrong type of people
**Example:** Hormozi's content "started attracting more wantrepreneurs. This was not my goal" (p.21)
**Response:** Recenter content around ideal customer; accept some audience loss

### Exception 5: Cool Factor vs. Economics
**Trigger:** Superior economics but losing to "cooler" competitor
**Example:** Hormozi supplement company - "I had put my entire budget into commissions and product, but none into building a brand people would think was cool" (p.2)
**Response:** Allocate budget specifically to brand building, not just product/economics

### Key Metrics to Track
```
BRAND HEALTH:
├── Reach (advertising metric)
│   └── How many people know about your stuff
├── Influence (branding metric #1)
│   └── Likelihood of changing behavior
└── Direction (branding metric #2)
    └── Towards or away from purchase

BUSINESS IMPACT:
├── CAC change over time (should decrease)
├── LTV change over time (should increase)
├── Pricing power (can you raise prices?)
├── Retention rate (do they come back?)
└── Referral rate (do others recommend?)
```

## Appendix B: What Branding Enables

### When You Do This Right (p.30)
- Get higher click through rates on all advertising
- Get higher closing rates
- Close at higher prices
- Get people to keep buying again and again
- Get cheaper customers than everyone else
- Get increasingly large customers over time
- Get new customers from compounding word of mouth
- Attract the best talent (they want to work for the best)
- Build an asset that itself has value

### When You Do This Wrong (p.30-31)
- Ward off good customers
- Attract bad ones
- Pay more for customers than you should
- Negative word of mouth compounds faster than any marketing
- Lose customers on the back end
- Need crazy new offers constantly to get customers
- Struggle to get good talent
- Constantly consider changing businesses
